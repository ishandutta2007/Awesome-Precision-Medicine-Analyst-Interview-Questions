# 4. Clinical & EHR Data Analysis

Working with clinical and electronic health record data — the other major data domain a precision medicine analyst needs to be fluent in, alongside genomics.

---

### 4.1 🟢 What is an Electronic Health Record (EHR), and why is EHR data generally considered messier and more challenging to analyze than data from a purpose-built research database or clinical trial dataset?

An EHR is the digital system used by healthcare providers to document and manage a patient's clinical information — diagnoses, medications, lab results, clinical notes, and procedures — primarily designed to support direct clinical care documentation and billing workflows, not primarily designed as a research or analytics data source. This is why EHR data is generally considered messier and more challenging to analyze than a purpose-built research dataset — clinical documentation practices vary significantly between providers and institutions, data is frequently entered in unstructured free-text clinical notes rather than standardized structured fields, coding practices (discussed further in this category) can be inconsistent or influenced by billing incentives rather than pure clinical accuracy, and the data reflects real-world clinical care patterns (with all their variability, incompleteness, and inconsistency) rather than the standardized, protocol-driven data collection of a controlled research study — a precision medicine analyst working with EHR data needs to understand and actively account for these data quality realities rather than treating EHR data with the same assumed reliability as carefully curated research data.

---

### 4.2 🟡 What is the difference between structured and unstructured clinical data, and what analytical techniques are typically needed to extract usable information from unstructured clinical notes?

Structured clinical data is entered into predefined, standardized fields with a fixed, machine-readable format (a lab test result value, a coded diagnosis, a medication order with defined dosage fields), directly usable for quantitative analysis without further processing. Unstructured clinical data consists of free-text narrative content (physician progress notes, radiology reports, pathology reports) that captures rich clinical detail and nuance but isn't directly machine-readable or analyzable in its raw form. Extracting usable information from unstructured clinical notes typically requires natural language processing techniques — ranging from simpler rule-based or keyword/pattern-matching approaches (searching for specific terms or patterns known to indicate a clinical concept of interest) to more sophisticated named entity recognition and clinical NLP models (specifically trained or adapted to accurately identify clinical concepts, medications, and relationships within the particular linguistic patterns and abbreviations common in clinical documentation, which differs substantially from general-purpose natural language) — and it's important to recognize that unstructured data extraction is inherently a probabilistic, imperfect process (introducing its own error and uncertainty into any resulting analysis) rather than as reliable as directly using already-structured data, meaning validation of extraction accuracy against a manually-reviewed reference sample is an important, non-optional step before relying on NLP-extracted data for any analysis with real clinical or research consequences.

### 4.3 🟢 What is ICD coding, and what are its known limitations as a source of "ground truth" diagnostic information for precision medicine research?

ICD (International Classification of Diseases) coding is a standardized system for representing diagnoses and health conditions using structured codes, widely used throughout healthcare systems primarily to support billing and administrative reporting, but also commonly used as a convenient, readily available source of structured diagnostic information for research and analytics purposes given how difficult unstructured note extraction (per category 4.2) can be. Its known limitations as a "ground truth" diagnostic source include the reality that ICD codes are often selected primarily to support accurate billing and reimbursement rather than to comprehensively or precisely capture the full clinical diagnostic picture (a code might be selected because it's the code that satisfies billing requirements for a given encounter, not necessarily because it's the single most clinically precise or complete descriptor of the patient's actual condition), coding practices and specificity can vary meaningfully across providers and institutions, and a code being present in a patient's record doesn't always reliably indicate the patient definitively has the associated condition (a code might reflect a condition being actively ruled out or considered in a differential diagnosis, rather than confirmed) — meaning a rigorous precision medicine analyst should treat ICD-code-based diagnostic identification as a useful but imperfect proxy requiring validation (ideally against actual clinical chart review for at least a representative sample) rather than as an automatically reliable, ground-truth diagnostic label.

### 4.4 🟡 What is the concept of a "phenotype algorithm" or "computable phenotype," and why is developing one typically more involved than simply querying for a single relevant diagnosis code?

A computable phenotype (or phenotype algorithm) is a formally defined, typically multi-criteria algorithm for identifying patients who genuinely have a specific clinical condition or characteristic of interest within EHR data, generally combining multiple data elements (diagnosis codes, medication history, relevant lab values, sometimes supplemented with NLP-extracted information from clinical notes) rather than relying on any single data element in isolation. This is typically more involved than simply querying for a relevant diagnosis code specifically because of the ICD coding limitations discussed in category 4.3 — a single diagnosis code alone is often neither sufficiently sensitive (missing genuinely affected patients whose condition wasn't captured through that specific code) nor sufficiently specific (incorrectly including patients who received the code for administrative reasons unrelated to a confirmed diagnosis) to reliably identify a genuine patient cohort for research or clinical purposes — a well-constructed computable phenotype algorithm typically requires iterative development and validation (checking the algorithm's identified patient cohort against manual clinical chart review for a representative sample to assess actual sensitivity and specificity) before it can be confidently used to define a patient population for a precision medicine research question or clinical application.

### 4.5 🟢 What is the difference between a "prospective" and "retrospective" clinical data analysis, and why does this distinction matter for the kinds of conclusions that can be responsibly drawn?

A prospective analysis defines its research question, patient population, and data collection plan before the relevant clinical events or outcomes occur, following patients forward in time according to a pre-specified protocol. A retrospective analysis instead uses data that was already collected (typically as part of routine clinical care, as with EHR data) for events that have already occurred, defining the research question and analytical approach after the fact. This distinction matters significantly for the kinds of conclusions that can be responsibly drawn because retrospective analyses are considerably more vulnerable to several forms of bias discussed further in category 5 (including the risk that a research question or specific analytical choices were shaped, even unconsciously, by already having seen how the data turned out, and confounding factors that a prospective study's design could have specifically controlled for but a retrospective analysis of already-collected routine care data often cannot fully address) — meaning retrospective EHR-based findings, however statistically significant they may appear, generally warrant more cautious interpretation and explicit acknowledgment of these inherent limitations compared to a well-designed prospective study, and often serve better as hypothesis-generating groundwork motivating a subsequent, more rigorous prospective study than as definitive, standalone evidence for clinical practice change.

### 4.6 🟡 What is immortal time bias, and how might it specifically arise in a retrospective precision medicine study examining outcomes associated with receiving a specific genomically-guided treatment?

Immortal time bias is a specific, well-documented methodological error that occurs when a period of follow-up time during which, by design or definition, the outcome of interest (often death, or treatment discontinuation) could not have occurred is inadvertently included and misclassified in a way that biases the analysis in favor of the treatment or exposure being studied. In a precision medicine context, this could arise, for example, in a retrospective study comparing outcomes between patients who received a specific genomically-guided targeted therapy versus those who didn't, if patients are classified into the "received targeted therapy" group based on treatment received at any point during their follow-up period, since a patient necessarily had to survive long enough to actually receive and begin that genomic testing and subsequent targeted therapy (which takes real time to arrange) — meaning the "received therapy" group's survival time before treatment initiation is sometimes inappropriately counted as "exposed" follow-up time in a way that artificially inflates that group's apparent survival benefit, since by definition, a patient couldn't have died during that pre-treatment window and still gone on to receive the treatment being studied. Correctly avoiding this bias generally requires careful time-dependent analytical approaches (such as treating treatment status as a time-varying covariate rather than a fixed baseline classification) rather than a simpler analysis that doesn't account for the timing of when treatment exposure actually began relative to the start of follow-up.

### 4.7 🟢 What is a common data model (like OMOP), and why has standardizing EHR data into a common data model become important for large-scale, multi-institutional precision medicine research?

A common data model is a standardized data structure and vocabulary that different healthcare institutions can map their own, often quite differently structured native EHR data into, enabling data from multiple different institutions (which may use entirely different EHR software systems and internal data conventions) to be analyzed using shared, consistent analytical tools and definitions without requiring each institution's data to be manually and separately reconciled for every new research question. This has become important for large-scale, multi-institutional precision medicine research because many clinically important questions — particularly those involving rare genetic conditions or rare gene-drug interactions — require aggregating data across many institutions to achieve adequate statistical power (a single institution's patient population is often simply too small to answer these questions reliably on its own), and common data models substantially reduce the practical technical and methodological burden of conducting this kind of multi-institutional research, which has directly enabled a growing number of large-scale research networks and consortia specifically built around common-data-model-standardized EHR data sharing.

### 4.8 🟡 What is data linkage, and what specific technical and privacy challenges arise when attempting to link a patient's genomic data with their longitudinal clinical/EHR data?

Data linkage refers to the process of connecting records belonging to the same individual across different data sources (linking a patient's genomic sequencing results, generated by a laboratory, to that same patient's longitudinal clinical history recorded in their EHR) so that combined genomic and clinical data can be analyzed together. Technical challenges include ensuring accurate, unambiguous patient matching across systems that may use different patient identifiers and may have data entry inconsistencies (a mismatched or duplicated patient identity across linked systems can introduce serious errors, incorrectly combining one patient's genomic data with a different patient's clinical history), and managing the different data update cadences and formats between a typically static, one-time genomic result and a continuously evolving longitudinal clinical record. Privacy challenges (discussed further in category 8) include the reality that linked genomic-clinical data is considerably more re-identifiable and sensitive than either data type in isolation (since the combination of detailed genomic information and detailed longitudinal clinical history creates a much richer, more uniquely identifying data profile than either alone), requiring particularly careful access control, governance, and consent frameworks specifically calibrated to this heightened sensitivity.

### 4.9 🟢 What is a clinical laboratory information system (LIS) versus an EHR, and why does a precision medicine analyst often need to work across both systems rather than finding all relevant data within a single source?

A laboratory information system is a specialized system used by a clinical laboratory to manage the testing workflow and results for lab-based tests (including genomic tests), often technically separate from the broader hospital or health system's primary EHR system, though results are typically eventually transmitted into the EHR for clinician access. A precision medicine analyst often needs to work across both systems because certain granular technical details relevant to interpreting a genomic result (specific sequencing metrics, detailed variant-level data beyond what's summarized in the clinical report, quality control flags) may be more readily and completely available within the laboratory's own LIS than within the more summarized, clinically-formatted version of the result that ultimately populates the EHR, while the broader clinical context needed to actually interpret a genomic finding's significance for a specific patient (their full diagnosis and treatment history, family history, other relevant clinical data) generally lives within the EHR rather than the LIS — meaning a comprehensive precision medicine analysis frequently requires understanding how to access, extract, and correctly link data across this kind of multi-system technical landscape, rather than assuming all relevant data can be found within any single system.

### 4.10 🟡 What is loss to follow-up, and why is it a particularly important methodological consideration for any precision medicine study examining long-term outcomes using real-world clinical data?

Loss to follow-up refers to patients who, for various reasons, stop being observed or having their data captured within a study's data source before the study's outcome of interest could be observed or the full intended follow-up period is complete — a patient might switch to a different healthcare system or insurance provider (meaning their subsequent clinical data simply isn't captured in the original data source anymore), move geographically, or, for less benign reasons, potentially stop appearing in a dataset specifically because of an adverse outcome that itself affects the ability to continue being observed (like death, which prevents any further data capture and requires careful handling depending on whether death itself is a relevant outcome being studied or a competing risk affecting other outcomes of interest). This is a particularly important consideration for real-world clinical data specifically because, unlike a tightly-controlled prospective clinical trial with deliberate mechanisms for actively tracking and following up with every enrolled participant, real-world EHR-based data capture is entirely dependent on a patient continuing to receive care within the data-capturing health system, meaning loss to follow-up in real-world data is often substantial and, critically, may not be random or unrelated to the outcome of interest (patients who feel better might be less likely to continue frequent healthcare visits generating ongoing data, while patients experiencing a poor outcome outside the observed health system, such as being hospitalized elsewhere, might simply disappear from the dataset without their outcome ever being captured at all) — potentially introducing significant, hard-to-fully-correct bias into any resulting analysis if not carefully considered and appropriately addressed through sensitivity analyses or explicit acknowledgment of this limitation.

### 4.11 🟢 What is the difference between a lab result's reference range and a clinically meaningful threshold, and why might a precision medicine analyst need to be cautious about treating a standard lab reference range as a universal, one-size-fits-all clinical cutoff?

A lab result's reference range typically reflects the statistically-derived range of values observed in a broadly defined "healthy" reference population (often defined as covering the central 95% of values observed in that reference group), while a clinically meaningful threshold for a specific purpose (like a treatment decision threshold, or a threshold indicating meaningfully elevated disease risk) is ideally derived from evidence specifically linking a given value to a specific clinical outcome or decision point, which isn't necessarily the same thing as the boundary of a generic statistical reference range. A precision medicine analyst should be cautious about assuming a standard reference range boundary is automatically the clinically appropriate threshold for a specific precision medicine application, particularly because reference ranges are sometimes derived from reference populations that may not adequately represent the specific patient population or clinical context relevant to a given precision medicine analysis (a general population reference range may not be well-calibrated for a specific disease population, a specific age or demographic group, or a specific analytical purpose like a genomically-informed treatment-monitoring threshold), meaning a genuinely rigorous analysis should seek out and use thresholds specifically validated for the actual clinical decision at hand, rather than defaulting to a generic reference range boundary simply because it's conveniently available and pre-established.

### 4.12 🟡 What is real-world evidence (RWE), and how does its role in precision medicine and regulatory decision-making differ from and complement traditional randomized controlled trial evidence?

Real-world evidence refers to clinical evidence derived from the analysis of real-world data (EHR data, insurance claims data, patient registries, and similar sources reflecting actual, routine clinical practice) rather than from the highly controlled, protocol-driven data collection of a traditional randomized controlled trial. Its role in precision medicine and regulatory decision-making has grown significantly (including formal recognition and evolving guidance from regulatory bodies like the FDA regarding when and how real-world evidence can support regulatory decisions) because RWE offers genuine complementary value that trials often can't provide on their own — much larger and more diverse patient populations reflecting real-world clinical heterogeneity (relevant to a field specifically concerned with how treatment effects vary across different patient subgroups), the ability to study rare genetic conditions or rare gene-drug combinations where a dedicated randomized trial may be impractically difficult to recruit for, and the ability to study long-term, real-world outcomes and treatment patterns beyond a trial's typically more limited follow-up period — but RWE generally cannot fully substitute for randomized controlled trial evidence when establishing a treatment's fundamental causal efficacy, given the confounding and bias vulnerabilities discussed throughout this category that are inherent to non-randomized, retrospective data, meaning RWE and RCT evidence are generally best understood as complementary evidence types serving different purposes within a comprehensive evidence base, rather than RWE being a simple lower-cost substitute for rigorous trial evidence.

### 4.13 🟢 What is a clinical data warehouse, and how does its typical purpose and structure differ from an operational, transaction-focused EHR system?

A clinical data warehouse is a separate data infrastructure specifically designed and structured to support analytical querying and research use of clinical data, typically consolidating and reorganizing data extracted from one or more operational source systems (like the EHR) into a structure optimized for the kind of complex analytical queries research and analytics work requires, rather than the transactional, real-time clinical workflow support an operational EHR system is primarily designed and optimized for. This structural difference matters practically because directly running complex, resource-intensive analytical queries against a live operational EHR system (which needs to remain fast and responsive for actual, real-time clinical care use) can be both technically problematic (potentially degrading the EHR's performance for actual clinical use) and is often simply not well-suited to the kind of broad, longitudinal, cross-patient analytical queries research work typically requires — meaning a precision medicine analyst working with institutional clinical data typically interacts primarily with a purpose-built clinical data warehouse (or a specific research-oriented data extract derived from one) rather than querying the live operational EHR system directly.

### 4.14 🔴 A retrospective analysis using your institution's EHR data finds a strong, statistically significant association between a specific genetic variant and improved response to a particular treatment. Before considering this a reliable, actionable finding, what specific concerns would you want to systematically rule out or address?

I'd want to work through a systematic checklist reflecting the major methodological vulnerabilities discussed throughout this category, rather than treating a single, statistically significant retrospective finding as sufficient evidence on its own. First, I'd want to carefully assess confounding — is it plausible that patients carrying this variant differ systematically from those who don't in ways beyond the variant itself that could independently explain the observed outcome difference (different baseline disease severity, different access to or timing of care, different comorbidities), and were these confounders adequately measured and controlled for in the analysis, given that unmeasured confounding is a persistent and often unresolvable limitation of retrospective, non-randomized data. Second, I'd want to specifically check for the kind of time-related biases discussed in category 4.6 (immortal time bias) and rule out reverse causation or other timing-related artifacts in how exposure and outcome were defined and measured. Third, I'd want to assess data quality and completeness for both the genomic and outcome variables involved (was variant status determined through a validated, high-quality testing and interpretation pipeline per category 2-3, and was the outcome measure reliably and consistently captured across the full patient population being studied, or does it depend on the kind of unstructured note extraction discussed in category 4.2 that introduces its own uncertainty). Fourth, I'd want to consider multiple testing and the broader analytical context this specific finding emerged from — was this association specifically hypothesized and tested as a single, pre-specified question, or did it emerge from a broader, more exploratory analysis examining many possible variant-outcome associations, which substantially increases the likelihood of encountering a statistically significant but ultimately spurious finding purely by chance (discussed further in category 5). And finally, I'd want to understand whether this finding has any independent, corroborating support from prior published literature or biological plausibility, or whether it would represent an entirely novel, unreplicated finding — before recommending any actual change to clinical practice based on this finding, I'd advocate strongly for independent replication (ideally in a different, independent dataset or patient population) and, where the potential clinical significance and feasibility justify it, a properly designed prospective study specifically designed to test this hypothesis with appropriate control for the confounding and bias concerns a retrospective analysis alone cannot fully resolve.
